Human Geography

Human geography is all about how the people and the land connect together into one world. The different areas of the world all bring their own uniqueness to the table. With different resources, landscapes, and culture the individual regions create a sense of originality. Human Geography is also the study the people's and resources' spatial organization. These places are very dynamic with changing land features and political boundaries. These changes may be caused by the residents or the environment. A region's values, attitudes, and behaviors may rub off on other regions. These changes on the other cultures could be positive or negative. Places can also exhibit a symbolic presence. An example of this would be how the Statue of Liberty on Ellis Island represents liberty. All of the locations are dependent on one another, creating interdependence. An example of interdependence is how we trade with many countries for their resources. We depend on the Middle East for their oil. (De Blij, Fouberg, Murphy,8)

Concepts of Culture

There are many different cultures with many different belief systems and values. These belief systems can identify a group of people. There are many examples of these belief systems. One is ethnocentrism which is the belief that all other cultures are inferior to your own. Imperialism is when a country extends their political or economic power over another culture or region. This may be direct through colonialism or indirect through trade. A culture that believes in masculinism believes that males are the predominant sex. Environmental determinism is the belief that the land affects the human activity in the area. (Knox, Marston, 3)

Regional vs. Systematic

Regional Geography focuses on boundaries or analytical constructs. This is based off of landscape language, religion, political systems, and economical systems. Systematic Geography is the study of topics. These topics may be population, religion, language, and economic development. (Knox, Marston, 4)

Spatial Analysis

There are many ways to identify a place or location. Nominal location is when we indicate a place by giving it a name. Absolute location is referring to a place with degrees in latitude and longitude. Relative location is directions in reference to other locations. A site is physical descriptions of the location such as soil, vegetation, usage,etc. Situation is similar to relative location.


There are corresponding ways to get to the location. Absolute distance is measured in miles or kilometers. Relative direction is measured in time or amount of money it takes to get there. Distance may be a mental perception, or an opinion of how far a place may be. Friction of distance is the fact that if a location is too far away, many people will not travel there due to the lack of ease or money needed. Distance-delay function is the idea that the farther away from a central point, the decrease of activity becomes more apparent. Topological Space is the connections between specific points. Accessibility is the measure of ease to get from one point to another. (De Blij, Fouberg, Murphy, 10)

The Five Themes

The first theme is location. The point of place creates a reaction and a reason to why that reaction happened. Location is practically the underlying concept of all Human Geography because of this. Geographers can use location to predict events or understand why past event occurred. This is called Location Theory.


The next is Human-Environment Interactions. The environment affect human actions through resource location, dangers, and pleasing climates. People affect the environment through pollution, altering ecosystems, and damaging resources.


The third theme is regions. Geographic phenomenas are not dispersed across Earth evenly. Regions may be may be agglomerated into different areas which are termed regions.


The next theme is place. A sense of place is remembering events and connecting memories with emotions about a place. A perception of a place is a feeling towards a location based on media.


The last theme is movement which can be described as the mobility of humans, products, and concepts. Spatial interaction depends on accessibility. (et al, 11)

Culture is the similar beliefs and values of a group of people. These values or other interests are considered cultural traits. The combination of many cultural traits is called a culture complex. A cultural hearth is where these culture traits originated. (et al, 26)

Diffusion

Culture diffusion is the spread of a culture trait from its hearth. Distance and time may inhibit or decrease diffusion. This is termed distance decay. Cultural barriers may totally inhibit the culture traits because of their own belief systems. Expansion diffusion is when the idea or product stays popular in the hearth while diffusing outward. Contagious diffusion is when almost all of the adjacent communities are using the product or idea. Hierarchical diffusion is when the trait is first passed to influential people or places. It then follows the places in the direction of rank. Stimulus diffusion is when the trait is directly introduced to the new community. Relocation diffusion is the movement of the people who have this trait and directly transport it to a new location. (et al, 28)

Boundary Process

The process of creating a boundary includes four steps: definition, often a treaty-like document that records the actual points of the landscape or describes the absolute location of the boundaries; delimitation, the actual drawing of the state's boundaries on a map by cartographers; demarcation, the physical bordering of the state with steel posts, concrete pillars, fences, or walls (often impractical or too expensive); and administration, which is how states regulate and maintain these boundaries.

Core/periphery Model

The world economy is a three-tier structure: the core, the periphery, and semi-periphery. Core processes incorporate higher levels of education and include higher wages and more technology. There processes develop more wealth in the global economy. On the contrary, periphery processes include the lower levels of education and incorporate lower salaries and less technology. Semi-peripheries, as you can imagine, are simply a buffer between the two extremes of core and periphery. This process generally includes the exploitation of the periphery by the core. Although these are processes, they occur in places and are sometimes assigned to states. For example, the US and Canada are core states while Chile and Iraq are periphery.

Territoriality

Human territoriality is a term generally referring to the opinion of Robert Sack who describes human territoriality as "the attempt by an individual or group to affect, influence, or control people, phenomena, and relationships, by delimiting and asserting control over a [specific] geographic area (De Blij et. al., 2007, p.222)." There are several ways for a group to control another, including physical force or organized sets of laws. His view contrasts with that of Robert Audrey, who believes that human territoriality is similar to the general behavior of animals. By contrast, Sack believes that human territoriality is shown in many arrangements based on the settings (as the territorial strategy changes) and should not be compared to mere animal instinct. Human behavior is learned unlike animals where behavior is instinctive. Central to the idea of state is the concept of territoriality, making states such as the USA or Portugal examples of a territoriality. The concept of territoriality today is tied closely with the concept of sovereignty; In specific terms, a territoriality is defined by a country or community's strong defense and protection over its territory as a result of its sense of attachment to it.
