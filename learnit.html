Ravenstein's Gravity Model


Ernst Ravenstein developed the Gravity Model. The Gravity Model predicts the interaction between two places based on the size of their populations and the distance between them. This model assumes that spatial interaction is directly linked to the populations and indirectly linked to the distance between the two places. There is a math equation that goes with the Gravity Model: multiple the two populations together and then divide by the distance between them. This equations shows how often they interact with each other.

Distance Decay


Distance decay is the idea that the farther two groups of people are away from each other, the communication between them decreases. Distance decay is commonly shown on a graph. One axis is labeled amount of interaction intensity, and the other axis is labeled increasing distance. At the top of the graph is "very high interaction." This means the distance between the two groups is small. Under "very high interaction" is "high interaction." The distance line shows the distance between the two groups is a little bigger. As you go down the graph, there becomes less interaction and a larger distance between the two groups.

Epidemiological Transition Model


The Epidemiological Transition Model shows the cause of death in each of the stages in the Demographic Transition Model. The first stage was developed by Abdel Omran in 1971. It has been modified to 4 main stages and a possible fifth stage. The Black Plaque was the major cause of death in Stage 1. It killed 25 million Europeans from 1347 to 1350, and the Black Plaque killed 13 million Chinese in 1380. Cholera, an infection in the small intestine, was a major epidemic in the time of the Industrial Revolution. This was the major epidemic in Stage 2. It killed a half of million people in New York City in 1832, and it wiped out ? of Cairo in 1831. In Stage 3, the major cause of death was infectious diseases and chronic disorders associated with aging, such as cardiovascular diseases and cancer. Infectious diseases have started to decline sharply in Stage 3 countries. Stage 4 is characterized by cardiovascular diseases and cancer. Medical advances have extended the life expectancy of older people. The growth of cancer can now be slowed down or removed all together. There is a possible Stage 5 that is characterized by infectious diseases and parasitic diseases.

Population Pyramid


Population pyramids show the age distribution and sex ratio of a specific population. They present the percentage of each age group in the entire population. Each age group is called a cohort. By looking at a country's population pyramid, you can tell how wealthy a country is and what stage of the DTM it is in. The key is to look at birth and death rates. In poorer countries, the birth and death rates are usually high. If there is a large amount of children and a small amount of elderly people, the country is usually less developed. In a wealthier country, the birth and death rates are low. If there is an almost equal percentage of children and elderly people, the country is usually more developed.

Core/Periphery Model (World Systems Theory)


The world economy is a three-tier structure: the core, the periphery, and semi-periphery. Core processes incorporate higher levels of education and include higher wages and more technology. There processes develop more wealth in the global economy. On the contrary, periphery processes include the lower levels of education and incorporate lower salaries and less technology. Semi-peripheries, as you can imagine, are simply a buffer between the two extremes of core and periphery. This process generally includes the exploitation of the periphery by the core. Although these are processes, they occur in places and are sometimes assigned to states. For example, the US and Canada are core states while Chile and Iraq are periphery.

Demographic Transition Model


The Demographic Transition Model (DTM) was developed in 1929 by Warren Thompson. It has four main stages. Stage 1 is low growth, Stage 2 is high growth, Stage 3 is moderate growth, and Stage 4 is low growth. In Stage 1, the crude birth rates (CBR) and the crude death rates (CDR) are equally high. The Bubonic Plaque marks Stage 1 because of its high CDR. There are no countries currently in Stage 1. In Stage 2, the CBR is high, but the CDR is declining. During the late 1700s-early 1800s, the world population increased greatly. This was also the time of the Industrial Revolution. In Stage 3, the CDR still decline but much slower. The CBR sharply starts to decline. Most countries in Europe and North America enter Stage 3 in the early 1900s. The world's population still grew, but it grew at a much slower rate. In Stage 4, both the CBR and the CDR are low. Stage 4 also signifies women becoming more educated and entering the work force. Depending on the version of the DTM you look at, there may or may not be a fifth stage. Though not an official stage, it is still possible. In Stage 5, the CBR and the CDR are equally low. This signifies zero growth.

Rostow's Model of Development


Walt Rostow's Ladder of Development model, also called the modernization model, shows the steps of development in countries. His model has to assume that all countries develop the same. There are five stages of development in this model. The first stage is called traditional. The main activity in countries at this stage is subsistence farming. These countries have a rigid social structure. The people of the country often refuse technological change. The second stage is called preconditions of takeoff. In this stage, there is a new leadership that moves the country towards diversity and flexibility. It prepares the country for the next stage. The third stage is called takeoff. During this stage, the country goes through something similar to an industrial revolution. Urbanization and industrialization start to occur. Improvements in technology and productions start to happen. The economy then enters the fourth stage called drive to maturity. During this stage, modernization starts to spark in important parts of the country. New technologies begin to diffuse, and industrial specialization starts up throughout the nation. Also, the population growth rate starts to slow. Only some countries reach the fifth stage. This stage is characterized by higher worker incomes and a widespread production of goods and services. The service industry develops greatly. Much of the country's workforce will be concentrated in the service sector. Rostow's model has received criticisms just like other development models. The problem with his model is it doesn't provide any context to development.

Lösch's Model


In 1967, August Lösch wanted to determine the where the best sites for building manufacturing plants were in order to maximize profit. He added the influence of consumer demands and production costs to his calculations. Businesses usually locate themselves in an area where the can make a profit. This is how Lösch's model works. He said the ideal place for a manufacturing plant would be in an area where production and transportation costs were low and income is high.

Hotelling's Model


Harold Hotelling created the locational interdependence model. It states that two business competitors will try to take away from each other's territories in order to maximize sales. This would eventually result in the two businesses selling their products across from each other. To explain this, Hotelling used two ice cream vendors selling ice cream on a beach. They both start by selling at opposite ends of the beach. After a while, both vendors try to maximize their sales by moving closer to the customers at the middle of the beach. Eventually, these vendors end up selling across from each other. The whole point of Hotelling's analysis was to show that the location of a business cannot be fully understood until it is referenced with the location of other businesses just like it.

Weber's Model


Alfred Weber, a German economic geographer, developed a model for the ideal location of a business. His model was called the Least Cost Theory. The Least Cost Theory finds the ideal location of a business by using three categories of costs that would minimize costs. These categories are transportation, labor, and agglomeration. The first category is transportation. The location had to result in the lowest possible cost of shipping raw materials to the manufacturing plant and the finished goods to the market. The second category is labor. Putting a business in an area where labor is cheap is ideal. If the business is put far from the resource location, the low cost of labor would make up for the transportation costs. The third category is agglomeration. Agglomeration is when many businesses are clustered in one area. This saves money because the equipment and furniture one company needs can be made by another company in the same area. The costs of transportation is greatly cut down.

Central Place Theory


The central place theory was created by Walter Christaller. This model predicts how and where central places in the urban hierarchy (cities to villages) would be distributed based on their function and placement. The model is based on a few assumptions. The first is that the surface of the ideal region would be flat with no physical barriers, such as mountains or rivers. Secondly, that the soil fertility would be universal, so agriculture could take place anywhere in the city. Third, population and purchasing power would be evenly distributed. Next, transportation would be uniform and be available from one region to the next. Finally, any good or service could be sold anywhere and in every direction out to a certain distance. The model is set up so the largest central place would provide the most functions to the majority of the region. His model isn't circular, but actually a collection of hexagons that fit perfectly together. You can see an example of Christaller's model in the Sunbelt phenomenon in southern regions.

House Structures


The architecture of homes vary as they are scattered across different locations in different environments. For example, most New England Homes have the chimney in the center as to warm the house as a whole. The Middle Atlantic has a warmer climate, therefore the chimney is at only one end of the house. Southern houses are raised off the ground to cool the interior.

Sector Model


The sector model was created by Homer Hoyt in the late 1930s. It serves as an evolved model from the concentric zone model, surpassing the limits of Burgess's. This model focuses on residential patterns to explain where the wealthy in a city choose to live. Hoyt developed this model like pie, with each slice representing a different residential area, education and recreation, transportation, and industrial sectors. The focal point of the city is the core, where the other sectors grow outward.

Multiple Nuclei Model


Chauncy Harris and Edward Ullman created the multiple nuclei model in the 1940s on the basis that neither the concentric zone or sector model adequately reflect mid-twentieth century cities. The theme of their model is that the CBD is losing its dominancy as the single core of a city.

Boundary Process


The process of creating a boundary includes four steps: definition, often a treaty-like document that records the actual points of the landscape or describes the absolute location of the boundaries; delimitation, the actual drawing of the state’s boundaries on a map by cartographers; demarcation, the physical bordering of the state with steel posts, concrete pillars, fences, or walls (often impractical or too expensive); and administration, which is how states regulate and maintain these boundaries.

Population Changes


Population changes are most commonly measured by the crude birth rate the crude death rate, and the rate of natural increase. The crude birth rate, or CBR, is total number of lives births annually per 1,000 people in the area being looked at. For example, if a country's CBR is 35, this means that for every 1,000 people in the country, there are 35 babies born over a one-year period in that country. The crude death rate, or CDR, is the total number of deaths in a one-year period per 1,000 people in the area being looked at. The CDR of a country is expressed in the same way as the countryÃ¢â‚¬â„¢s CBR. If a country's CDR is 15, it is expressed as 15 deaths a year per 1,000 people living in that country. The word crude is used because these rates are calculated in a society, not certain individuals or groups. The rate of natural increase, or RNI, is the percentage of population growth in a year. It is found by subtracting the CDR from the CBR. For example, if the CBR is 35 births per 1,000 people and the CDR is 15 deaths per 1,000 people, then the RNI is 20 per 1,000, or 2.0%. The RNI excludes migration (Rubenstein, 2005, pg. 53).

Currently, the worldÃ¢â‚¬â„¢s RNI is 1.3. This means that every year, the world's population is growing 1.3%. This rate is not nearly as high as it was in 1963 at 2.2%, the all-time high record. The RNI has dropped sharply in the past few decades. The world population is growing by 80 million people annually. The annual increase has dropped at a much slower rate than the RNI because the population base is much higher than it was in the past. For example, the world's population increased from 4 billion to 5 billion people in just 13 years, and the world's population increased from 5 billion to 6 billion in just 12 years. The population base continues to grow so much that a one-tenth of 1% change could dramatically change the world's population growth. The difference between a 1% and 2% change is huge because of the high population base. The difference between a RNI of 1.0% and a RNI of 1.3% is 15 million more people are added to the world's population with a RNI of 1.3% than a RNI of 1.0% (Rubenstein, 2005, pgs. 53-57).

Another way to calculate the total number of births in a society is the total fertility rate, or TFR. TFR is the average number of children a will give birth to during her childbearing years (generally 15 to 50). The TFR is more of a prediction of how many children will be born per woman in the future. The TFR is calculated by assuming that women in the future will have the same number of babies that women in the present will have. Currently, the global TFR is about 3. The TFR is generally higher in less developed countries and lower in more developed countries. Beside CDR, to other forms of mortality measurements are infant mortality rate, or IMR, and life expectancy. IMR is the number of deaths annually of children under the age of one. It is expressed as the number of deaths per 1,000 births. The higher IMRs are in poorer countries, such as sub-Saharan African countries. The lower IMRs are found in Europe and other more developed countries. IMR usually mirror the countryÃ¢â‚¬â„¢s health care system. Countries with more well-trained doctors and nurses and more modern hospitals and medicines have lower IMR than countries with less well-trained doctors and nurses and less modern hospitals and medicines. Life expectancy is another form of mortality measurement. Life expectancy is the total number of years an individual is expected to live in the current conditions in which they live in. The life expectancy is higher in wealthier countries and lower in poorer countries (Rubenstein, 2005, pgs. 55-57).

Migration


There are three basic types of movement: cyclic movement, periodic movement, and migration. Cyclic movement is a journey that begins in one place and ends in that same place. Cyclic movement can be described as a daily routine. The space a person needs to function in during cyclic movement is called activity space. Commuting is a form of cyclic movement. Commuting is described as the journey from home to work and then back to home again. This can include many different forms of transportation, such as by bus, by train, and by car. Seasonal movement is another form of cyclic movement. An example of seasonal movement is living in the US for the winter and then returning to the Canada to live for the rest of the year. Nomadism is another form of cyclic movement. Although not very common, it still exists in parts of Africa and Asia. Nomadism is when nomads travel a long, familiar path over and over again. These nomads become familiar with the water and food sources along the path they travel (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs.71-72).

Periodic movement is a like cyclic movement; it is a journey away from home. The difference is periodic movement is a long period away from home while cyclic movement is a short journey away from home. A common form of periodic movement is migrant labor. Millions of workers temporarily move to places where cheap labor is needed. Transhumance is a system pastoral farming in which farmers move their livestock to different pastures depending on the season. It is considered a periodic movement because it involves a long time away from home for the farmer. Going away to college can be categorized as periodic movement. Even though you are keeping the same home address, most of your time is spent at the college. Military service is another form of periodic movement. It involves being temporarily relocated to a new place that can last several years (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, p.72).

Migration is defined as being moving permanently to a new location across large distances. International migration is the movement between country borders. When a migrant leaves the country they live in, they are called an emigrant, and they are called an immigrant when they enter a new country to live. Immigration adds to the population of a country, and emigration subtracts from the population of a country. Internal migration is migration that takes place inside a countryÃ¢â‚¬â„¢s borders. Internal migration varies due to the mobility of that country's population. Longer distances are more common in countries with a mobile population. In the US, internal migrants are more likely to move from one economic region to another. Because of this, there has been an increase in internal migration in the past few decades to the Sunbelt and Far West regions. In country's with a less mobile population, common internal migration is moving from the rural areas to the capital city of that country. Reasons for internal migration are economic opportunities, retirement, and the want for different lifestyles (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs.72-73).

Why People Migrate


Forced migration deals with a position of authority. This authority makes people move without giving them a choice, also known as involuntary migration. a good example of forced migration is the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade. Africans were taken from their homes and sent to the Americas and the Caribbean to be sold as slaves. Many of the Africans did not survive the boat ride from Africa. This was the largest practice of forced migration in history. The number of African sold into slavery is not known, but the estimates are anywhere from 12 million to 30 million. Africans were first sent to the Caribbean during the 16th century. Despite popular belief, most of the Africans were not sent to North America. The majority of them were sent to the Caribbean and South America (mainly Brazil). Forced migration still exists today in the form of countermigration. For example, in the 1990s, the US sent Haitian immigrants that arrived in Florida back to Haiti (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs.74-76).

Voluntary migration is migration in which the immigrants have a choice on if they want to move and to where they want to move. Push and Pull factors coincide with voluntary migration. Push factors are conditions that "push" people away from the place they are currently living. Pull factors are factors that attracts migrants to a certain location. Distance decay comes into the picture when talking about pull factors. Distance decay is the idea that the farther an individual or group away from another individual or group, the communication between them decreases. This idea can help people decide where they want to move to because they may not want to lose communication with another group by migrating far away. Step migration is moving to a certain destination in stages. An example of this could be moving a peasant living in the rural area of Brazil. His goal is to migrate to the capital city of Brasilia. First, he will most likely move to a small village, then to a town nearby. From the town, he will most likely move to a city, and then finally, he will move to Brasilia. However, not all migrants who set out for a large city will make it there. They may encounter employment along the way to the large city, or intervening opportunity (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs.74, 76-77).

There are different types of push and pull factors: economic conditions, political circumstances, armed conflict and civil war, environmental conditions, culture and traditions, and technological advances (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 77-79).

Economic Conditions- Many people face poverty in their home country. In search of a better life, many immigrants, both illegal and legal, cross country borders to look for new opportunities to find work (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 77).

Political Circumstances- Many immigrants migrate because of the way the government works in their country. Also, a certain government system may attract immigrants to a country (de Blij,Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 77).

Armed Conflict and Civil War- Many people cannot stay safe in their country during a war. This causes people to migrate to safer regions (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 77).

Environmental Conditions- Environmental conditions can mean anything from natural disasters to famines to pollution. This causes migration to another location that could support the group of people (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 77-78).

Culture and Traditions- The culture and traditions of a certain group of people differ from group to group. When a new political change takes over the government, they might not agree with the culture and traditions of a certain group. This may cause the group to leave the country and practice their culture and traditions somewhere else (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 78-79).

Technological Advances- Different areas of the world are more technologically advanced than other areas. In those areas, communication and transportation systems are more modern and easier to use. This attracts new immigrants to these areas (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 79).

Language is diffused by interaction between people. Long ago, languages were diffused by colonization and mercantilism. A lingua franca is a common language used by people who speak different languages for the purpose of trade and commerce. GNI is a limited measurement because it only includes the country's formal economy, or the monitored economy that is taxed by the government. The country’s informal economy, or the illegal or uncounted economy that isn't monitored or taxed. Informal economies can have a great impact on the country's overall economy (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs.302-303).

The development of technology and improvements in production is another way to measure the development of a country. You can take the percentages of the workers employed in different sectors of the economy. A less developed country has a high percentage of workers in the food production sector. A more developed country has a high percentage of workers employed in the technology and service industries. Also, a person's access to different modes of transportation and different forms of communication is another way of assessing the development of a country using technology (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 304).

The development of social welfare is another way to measure the development of a country. Dependency ratios are common ways to measure the development of social welfare in a country. A dependency ratio is a measure of the number of dependents that each 100 employed people must support. Usually, the lower the dependency ratio, the less economic and social strain there is. Some other methods of measuring the development of social welfare in a country are literacy rate, life expectancy, infant mortality, and amount of savings per capita (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 304).

Walt Rostow's Ladder of Development model, also called the modernization model, shows the steps of development in countries. His model has to assume that all countries develop the same. There are five stages of development in this model. The first stage is called traditional. The main activity in countries at this stage is subsistence farming. These countries have a rigid social structure. The people of the country often refuse technological change. The second stage is called preconditions of takeoff. In this stage, there is a new leadership that moves the country towards diversity and flexibility. It prepares the country for the next stage. The third stage is called takeoff. During this stage, the country goes through something similar to an industrial revolution. Urbanization and industrialization start to occur. Improvements in technology and productions start to happen. The economy then enters the fourth stage called drive to maturity. During this stage, modernization starts to spark in important parts of the country. New technologies begin to diffuse, and industrial specialization starts up throughout the nation. Also, the population growth rate starts to slow. Only some countries reach the fifth stage. This stage is characterized by higher worker incomes and a widespread production of goods and services. The service industry develops greatly. Much of the country's workforce will be concentrated in the service sector. Rostow's model has received criticisms just like other development models. The problem with his model is it doesn't provide any context to development (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 305).

Development and Geography


Development happens in context. Context is using various scales to tell what is happening to a country and then combining those scales. To understand why the wealthiness of a country is different than the wealthiness of another, you have to look at not only at it from a state scale, but also from local, regional, and global scales too. Today, many scholars argue that poorer countries are going through neo-colonialism. Neo-colonialism happens when major world powers control the economies of less developed and poorer countries, even though the poorer countries are independent states. Development scholars have produced a number amount of theories called structuralist theories. A structuralist theory explains that economic inequality was built into the world economy. The dependency theory was created by structuralists. It states that political and economical relationships between countries and regions control and limit the development possibilities for poorer countries or regions. An example of this is colonialism. Colonialism created political and economical structures that made the colonies become dependent on the colonial power. This dependency helps sustain the regions with more prosperity and poverty in other regions. This can still happen after decolonization occurs (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 305-308).

Immanuel Wallerstein's world-systems theory states that the social changes in the developing world are directly related to the economic activities of the developed world. Wallerstein divided the world economy into the three-tier structure. This consists of the core, periphery, and semi-periphery. Core processes are processes that generate wealth in a place. This is because these processes require greater technologies, higher levels of education, and higher salaries. When core processes are put in a certain place, it brings in wealth for the people of the community. Periphery processes are the opposite of core processes because require little education, lower technologies, and lower salaries. Core regions are regions that have reached high levels of socioeconomic prosperity. These regions are big players in the global economic game. Periphery regions are poor the depend greatly on the core. They do not have much control over their economical or political affairs. Semi-periphery combines both core and periphery processes. Places of semi-periphery act as a buffer between the core and periphery in the world economy. Semi-periphery countries have more power than periphery regions; however, core regions still have a large influence on semi-periphery countries.

The Barriers and Costs of Economic Development


The world economic system is a problem for peripheral countries, but it is not the only problem. There are other barriers they face that slow them from developing economically. These barriers are low levels of social welfare, foreign debt, political instability, and the widespread disease. Peripheral countries face social problems that make economic development hard to accomplish. From a demographic perspective, peripheral countries have high birth and death rates with a low life expectancy. In fact, over half the population in peripheral countries is either 15 or younger. This is a problem because there are less adult laborers and more dependents. The low life expectancy and high infant mortality rates come from not having adequate nutrition. Lack of education is also a big problem. Many school-aged children do not attend school because everyone has to pay to send their children to school, even the poor. Peripheral countries have some of the highest illiteracy rates in the world. Usually, boys attend school longer than girls. Trafficking is when adults and children are manipulated into working under conditions that they would normally not choose. These adults and children are often seeking a better life when they get manipulated into trafficking. Trafficking is not considered slavery because no one is being sold (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 309-310).

Foreign debt is a big problem for peripheral countries. International financial institutions lend money to newly independent countries in the 1960s. Private banks and lending firms in the United States, Europe, and Japan recently started to lend peripheral countries money. Starting in the 1980s, the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund started to loan very large amounts of money to peripheral countries for the purpose of development. These loans were given out in exchange for reforms in the county's economy or government, such as reducing tariffs and opening the country to foreign trade. These loans were called structural adjustment loans. Both the IMF and the World Bank required the countries they loaned money to to make structural adjustments in exchange for the loans. Now the peripheral countries owed money to these organizations. When a peripheral country spends a large portion of its budget on debt repayments, it has less money to spend of the development of the country. Many countries' debts (including interest) are more than their revenues. This created a debt crisis for the poorest countries in the world (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 310-311).

Political instability is a very common problem within peripheral countries. Political instability in these countries is a result of the competition between the wealthy for control of the government and the disenfranchisement of the poor. It is hard for peripheral countries to maintain political stability. This is because their democratic governments failed, and most were taken over by military coups. Many countries have gone back and forth between a democratic government to a military government. Experts say it is a democratic government cannot be maintained without wealth. Politicians usually become corrupt in areas where there is much poverty. When a government becomes corrupt, they cut off the development aid to country by spending the money for other reasons that do not help the country. Core countries and other nongovernmental organizations try to help the country when this happens, but it is usually never enough to meet the basic need. Many corrupt government leaders stay in power for decades because the people are intimidated by the leader and the leader's followers. Those that do speak out against leader are often either killed or harmed by the government. The countries that do manage to overthrow their dictator have a long road ahead of them to getting their country back on track (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 311-312).

Diseases are very common in peripheral countries. Diseases directly affect the development of the country’s economy by weakening the labor force and making survival a challenge. Vectored diseases are diseases that start with one host and get spread to other hosts. Vectored diseases infect many people in the periphery. These infectious diseases spread quickly throughout host populations infecting more people each day. Malaria is a very common vectored disease in the periphery. Malaria is a disease carried by mosquitoes. This disease is caused by a parasite in the mosquitoes' saliva. Every year, 2 to 3 million people die from malaria, most of them being under the age of 5. Malaria is all over the world,especially in sub-Saharan Africa, China, Southeast Asia, the tropical Americas, and in India. Other species of monkeys, rats, snakes, and birds can be infected with malaria. There are anti-malaria drugs on the market, but in order to stop it, the mosquitoes must be eliminated. The government of Sri Lanka, formerly known as Ceylon, launched a huge attack on mosquitoes in the 1940s using a pesticide called dichloro diphenyl trichloroethane, or DDT. The mosquitoes were almost wiped out, and the number of deaths associated with malaria significantly went down. DDT turned out to cause health and environmental problems, so it went out of use. Today there is a new tactic to fighting malaria. Special engineered mosquitoes are being released into the wild. These mosquitoes are genetically modified so that they can not transmit this disease anymore. The purpose of these mosquitoes is to see the number of mosquitoes that are not genetically modified decrease sharply. Also, in efforts to stop malaria, many programs have started to distribute mosquito nets so people are protected when they sleep at night (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 312-315).

Economic development is great for a country. However, there are still costs associated with economic development. This does not mean costs in money but more along the lines of physical costs. The three main areas of economic development that have costs associated with them. They are industrialization, agriculture, and tourism. The governments of many peripheral countries have set up special zones that focus on exporting called export processing zones, or EPZs. These EPZs offer favorable trade agreements, tax, and regulatory to foreign businesses. The two best known EPZs are the Mexican maquiladoras and the special economic zones in China. These zones are put near places that have easy access to export markets. The maquiladora zone is directly across from the United States-Mexico border. The special economic zones are located near ports. These zones attract a variety of manufacturing operations. The Mexican government designated the northern region of Mexico as the site for the maquiladora. They used this region so that the manufactured goods could be sent into the United States free of import tariffs. The maquiladora process began in the 1960s. It did not really take off until the 1980s. The wages were much lower in Mexico, so American manufacturing businesses began to relocate to the maquiladora region of Mexico. Today there are about 2000 manufacturing plants in this region. Over 600,000 people are employed by these plants. About 20% of Mexico’s industrial labor force was concentrated in the maquiladora region. Many environmental regulations are not followed resulting in air and water pollution in that region. In 1992, the North American Free Trade Agreement, or NAFTA, between the United States, Canada, and Mexico. NAFTA facilitated the movement of manufacturing plants and service industries to Mexico. The two districts where the new plants are located are Tijuana under San Diego and Ciudad Juarez across from El Paso, Texas (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 315).

Agriculture in peripheral countries can either mean personal consumption or large scale production for a large agricultural company. Agriculture has modernized in these countries for the large scale production for the foreign market. This does not make a large impact for the surrounding areas. The distribution systems are usually poorly organized so not much food is grown for the local market. The land owned by the local population is usually fragmented, or divided into small plot of land that makes it challenging to farm efficiently on. Most farmers have outdated equipment and tools that are very inefficient. The crops that are usually grown are grain and roots. This is because these crops produce a high yield. Fertilizers are luxuries that cannot be afforded. Also, the farmers know little about soil conservation. The common result of this is soil erosion. Countries that face severe soil erosion and have dry climates next to deserts are at high risk of desertification, or the process of desert expansion into farming lands. Desertification has hit Africa very hard. Most of the African continent is arid or semiarid. People have no choice but to farm in these conditions. Some farmers and ranchers have turned some of these arid lands and made them into lands that can be used for farming and ranching. These lands are generally used more intensively to increase agricultural productions. In the last 50 years, more than 270,000 miles of farming land have become part of the desert in sub-Saharan Africa. Humans are the main cause of the desertification. Soil exhaustion, overgrazing, woodcutting, and misuse have sped up this process in this region of the world (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 315-317).

Peripheral countries have become favorite tourist destinations among travelers for wealthier countries. Tourism has its ups and downs; however, the downs greatly outweigh the ups. Really, the only major upside to tourism is it is a source of revenue and employment to the country. The one of the downsides of tourism is the country has to invest in tourist attractions, hotels, and other infrastructure necessary for tourism. The materials used for building hotels and other tourist buildings takes away from building houses for the citizens of the country. The majority of the income that the country receives from hotels is from the tourists' consumption of food, water, and electricity. Most of the money is reinvested in building other infrastructure, such as cruise ports and airports. The tourism industry pays its employees low wages. Most have to work more than one job just to support themselves and their families. Many locals resent the wealthier tourists in their poorer societies. Tourism can also change the cultural landscape of a place. Huge hotels stand over the poorer housing that the locals live in. Also, hotels serve many meals a day while children are starving and suffering from malnutrition down the street. Even though tourism has many downsides to it, the local communities still rely on the tourist industry as a source of income (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 317-319).

Location Theories and Industrial Location


Primary activities are activities that deal with extracting resources from the Earth. Secondary activities are activities that manufacture the resources that were extracted. Improvements in transportation have made secondary activities less dependent on the resource location. Secondary industries don't have to be located next to the primary activities because raw materials can be transported over long distances. Now, manufacturers have to find the right place to put their business. A location theory predicts where a business should be located. Models for the location of secondary industries have to assume that the manufacturer wants to maximize their profit and take variable costs into account, such as energy costs transportation expenses. Friction of distance is a key issue in trying to maximize advantages. Friction of distance is when time and cost increase when the distance increases. An example of this is a raw material being shipped across the country. The the cost of shipping the material will go up, but you can sell the product for a greater price to make a profit (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 367-368).

Alfred Weber, a German economic geographer, developed a model for the ideal location of a business. His model was called the Least Cost Theory. The Least Cost Theory finds the ideal location of a business by using three categories of costs that would minimize costs. These categories are transportation, labor, and agglomeration. The first category is transportation. The location had to result in the lowest possible cost of shipping raw materials to the manufacturing plant and the finished goods to the market. The second category is labor. Putting a business in an area where labor is cheap is ideal. If the business is put far from the resource location, the low cost of labor would make up for the transportation costs. The third category is agglomeration. Agglomeration is when many businesses are clustered in one area. This saves money because the equipment and furniture one company needs can be made by another company in the same area. The costs of transportation is greatly cut down (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 368).

Harold Hotelling created the locational interdependence model. It states that two business competitors will try to take away from each other's territories in order to maximize sales. This would eventually result the two businesses selling their products across from each other. To explain this, Hotelling used two ice cream vendors selling ice cream on a beach. They both start by selling at opposite ends of the beach. After a while, both vendors try to maximize their sales by moving closer to the customers at the middle of the beach. Eventually, these vendors end up selling across from each other. The whole point of Hotelling's analysis was to show that the location of a business cannot be fully understood until it is referenced with the location of other businesses just like it (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 368).

In 1967, August Lösch wanted to determine the where the best sites for building manufacturing plants were in order to maximize profit. He added the influence of consumer demands and production costs to his calculations. Businesses usually locate themselves in an area where the can make a profit. This is how Lösch's model works. He said the ideal place for a manufacturing plant would be in an area where production and transportation costs were low and income is high (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 369).

Changes in Industrial Production


New innovations in the production process triggered a manufacturing boom in the 20th century. One of the more significant of these innovations was the mass-production assembly line invented by Henry Ford. The assembly line allowed for the mass-production of goods in a single place. Ford's idea was called the Fordist system. Fordist was basically a way of organizing labor and production. Many countries and corporations supported each other under the Fordist system. Today, the world economy follows a post-Fordist system. This system includes production practices that where the goods are not mass produced. Production is mainly outsourced to different countries around the world. Time-space compression is the idea that many regions of the world are more connected through different communication and transportation technologies. Fluctuations in one economy can affect another around the world in a few minutes. When regions of the world were more connected, goods were produced closer to where the demand was. Now, goods can be produced anywhere in the world and can be transported to where the demand is. Just-in-time delivery is when goods can be transported to corporations around the world when they are needed. This creates a global division of labor, or when corporations hire workers form all over the world. The global division of labor has changed the role of different economic sectors within a country's economy. For example, the agricultural industry has become very mechanized and very efficient. Also, many manufacturing companies have moved to semi-peripheral or peripheral countries. This results in core countries having a large labor force concentrated in the tertiary sector of the economy (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pgs. 377-378).

During the post-Fordist era, there were new influences on the location of industries besides low wages. They are intermodal transportation, regional and world trade agreements, and the availability of energy. Efficient modes if transportation allow manufacturers to purchase raw materials from a great distance and transport finished goods across a great distance. An intermodal connection is a location where two or more modes of transportation meet up. This reduces the cost of transportation as well as makes it easier to ship goods. The container system is a revolutionary invention in the transportation sector. It allows for goods to be shipped in large, metal containers. These containers can fit on many forms of transportation, such as cargo ships, trains, and trucks (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 379).

The trade agreements made by the WTO work to push the world into free trade. Most of the regional trade agreements try to do away with the quotas and tariffs between these member countries. Regardless of if it's a regional or a global trade agreement, it will affect what is produced in the area and the location of the production (de Blij, Murphy, & Fouberg, 2007, pg. 379).

Manufacturing plants have always been near the energy source that powers them. During the Industrial Revolution, many factories were built near coal fields for easy transportation of the coal. The 5 Hearths of Urbanization


A hearth is a point at which diffusion explodes outwards. Human geographers have defined five hearths as the basis of urbanization expansion. These hearths are Mesopotamia, the Nile River Valley, the Indus River Valley, the confluence of the Huang He and Wei River Valleys, and Mesoamerica. Each hearth has its own characteristics and traits that make it such a leader in urbanization.


Mesopotamia is the first hearth. It is located in an area of Southwest Asia called the Fertile Crescent. This hearth includes all the great cities between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. Mesopotamia exhibited social inequality, which meant that there was a separation between the upper and lower classes. The urban elite built walls around their palaces and hired artists to decorate their homes. The people of Mesopotamia created a priest-king class, which supported religious leaders, along with a religious-political ideology to support them. This meant that the rulers were in charge of both religion and government. These individuals had tasks like levying taxes and collecting part of the harvest presented by agricultural workers. Opposite of the urban elite were the ordinary citizens. Typical homes were packed together like mud-walled sardines, the only thing separating one from its neighbor was a narrow lane. These were better than the huts of poor citizens, smeared with mud and reeds. Workshops lined the roads, mud walls surrounded the city, and in the middle was an early type of CBD filled with houses and shrines. Temples were large buildings and were built on mounds of dirt over 100 feet high. There was a complete lack of sanitation, so disease was a major cause of death.


The Nile River Valley is the second hearth of urbanization that began around 3200 BC. Interestingly, archeologists debate whether the Nile River Valley is truly a hearth, or if it's just diffusion from Mesopotamia. It would appear as if the agricultural techniques and methods diffused from Mesopotamia. However, there is evidence that supports independent urbanization in this area. The introduction of irrigation made this hearth unique from the rest. There was also an absence of walls around the city. The Nile River Valley had singular control, where the power coincided with control of the irrigation system. In addition to the normal architecture, there were also the great pyramids, tombs, and the sphinx, all which were built by slaves.

The Placement of Cities


Cities around the world are located where they are for specific reasons and multiple factors. Urban geographers are always searching for these reasons, and they use many techniques. Using quantitative data, urban geographers sometimes look at the trade area of a city. The trade area is simply the region where a city has a dominant influence. Two other factors that affect a city's location are population and distance. Urban geographers have made conclusions about different sized cities called the rank-size rule. This rule states that the largest city in an area has the largest trade area due to the most transportation. Medium-sized cities trade in smaller areas of commerce and are scattered from the major city. Small towns house the grocery stores and other necessities. The smallest villages may have a small shop or gas station. A hierarchy forms based on population and trade area. The rank-size rule also states that the population of a city is inversely proportional to its hierarchical rank. The rank-size rule is not always present in countries that have a city that is much larger than the rest, like Paris or Mexico City. This rule doesn't state the location or placement of cities, though.


The central place theory was created by Walter Christaller. This model predicts how and where central places in the urban hierarchy (cities to villages) would be distributed based on their function and placement. The model is based on a few assumptions. The first is that the surface of the ideal region would be flat with no physical barriers, such as mountains or rivers. Secondly, that the soil fertility would be universal, so agriculture could take place anywhere in the city. Third, population and purchasing power would be evenly distributed. Next, transportation would be uniform and be available from one region to the next. Finally, any good or service could be sold anywhere and in every direction out to a certain distance. The model is set up so the largest central place would provide the most functions to the majority of the region. His model isn't circular, but actually a collection of hexagons that fit perfectly together. You can see an example of Christaller's model in the Sunbelt phenomenon in southern regions. Henry Ford said, in regard to the central place theory, "[The central place theory can still add] analytical power to the understanding of patterns of urban growth, even in this era of fast and long-distance transportation, suburbanization, and multiple urban functions."


Each city model shows functional zonation, or the practical division of a city into zones based on their function. There are many zones that are important to understand before looking at any models. The Central Business District(CBD) is a concentration of businesses and commerce in the center of a city. A CBD is usually associated with high real estate values, skyscrapers and tall buildings, traffic, converging highways, and mass transit systems. Central city is a term to describe a strictly urban area that has no suburban traits. The central city is sometimes synonymous with the older part of a city versus the newer suburbs. A suburb describes a uniform part of an urban area that is usually adjacent to the city. Suburbs are generally residential, but you can find other features like schools, malls, and office parks as well. Suburbanization is the spread of cities into surrounding areas that were previously uninhabited. It is typically a transition from rural to urban.


There are three models for the North American city. The first is the concentric zone model, created by Ernest Burgess. His model divides the city into 5 concentric zones based on their function. The CBD is in the center, then the zone of transition. A zone of independent workers' homes is third, surrounded by middle-class residents, and finally enclosed by the suburban ring. This model is dynamic, so as the city grows, the zones can change.


Homer Hoyt created the sector model in the late 1930s. This model focuses on residential patterns, explaining why the wealthy live where they do. The sector model is divided into pie slices, where each zone occupies a certain slice.


Chauncy Harris and Edward Ullman created the multiple nuclei model which was sort of a compromise between the sector and concentric zone models. This model shows the CBD is losing its dominancy as the main nucleus and proposes that suburban areas also have their own nuclei. However, even this model is said to be too simple for a modern city.
